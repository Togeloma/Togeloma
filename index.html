Frequenty Asked Questions

  • What is childhood cancer?

    Childhood cancer refers to any cancer that occurs in children and young adults under the age of 20. It can affect any part of the body and can be either malignant or benign.

  • How common is childhood cancer?

    Childhood cancer is relatively rare, accounting for less than 1% of all cancer diagnoses. However, it still affects thousands of families every year.

  • What are the most common types of childhood cancer?

    The most common types of childhood cancer are leukemia, brain and central nervous system tumors, lymphoma, and solid tumors such as neuroblastoma and Wilms tumor.

  • What are the signs and symptoms of childhood cancer?

    The signs and symptoms of childhood cancer can vary depending on the type and location of the cancer. Some common symptoms include unexplained weight loss, persistent fever, unusual lumps or swelling, fatigue, and unexplained pain.

  • How is childhood cancer treated?

    Childhood cancer is typically treated with a combination of surgery, chemotherapy, and radiation therapy. The specific treatment plan depends on the type and stage of the cancer.

  • What are the long-term effects of childhood cancer treatments?

    Childhood cancer treatments can have long-term side effects, such as increased risk of secondary cancers, organ damage, and developmental delays. Many childhood cancer survivors require ongoing medical care and monitoring to manage these long-term effects.

  • How can I support children with cancer and their families?

    There are many ways to support children with cancer and their families, such as volunteering at a local hospital, donating to childhood cancer research organizations, and raising awareness about childhood cancer.

  • How can I get involved with your organization?

    You can get involved with our organization by volunteering your time, making a donation, or attending one of our fundraising events. Please visit our website for more information on how to get involved.
